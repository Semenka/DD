# Founder pattern matching — what actually predicts outcome

The single strongest predictor of founder outcome that I have seen across hundreds of deals is "relentless resourcefulness". This is from Paul Graham but it remains true. It is not pedigree. It is not Stanford or Google. It is the willingness to push past N+1 rejections, to ship the unsexy version, to do customer support yourself in the first year, to delete the feature your engineer worked on for three months because it didn't work.

When I look at a founder, I am asking four questions:

1. What have they actually shipped — products, businesses, open source, public writing? Not what they say they want to do. What is already on the table.
2. How fast do they iterate when reality contradicts their plan? The founders who win update their model in days, not quarters.
3. Are they the right founder for this specific market, or are they a generic founder who has chosen this market? The difference is usually visible in why-this-why-now answers.
4. Do they pass the "would I work for them" test? Founders who cannot recruit talent that is better than they are at specific functions do not scale past Series A.

I am suspicious of founders who use a lot of adjectives. "We are building a world-class, AI-native, vertically integrated platform" is a sentence that should make you nervous. Strong founders use nouns and verbs. "We sell payroll software to dentists. We started with the dentist my co-founder's family used. We have 11 customers."

I am suspicious of founders who have not had a real failure. Failure is information. Founders who have only had wins have not been stress-tested.

I am most excited about founders who have a specific, narrow insight that they have lived with for years, and a wedge they can describe in a single sentence. The big TAM comes later. The wedge has to be sharp now.
