# Reading a cap table — who's on it and what it means

A cap table is a record of beliefs. Each investor on the table represents a specific bet that someone made at a specific moment with a specific information set. Reading the cap table well is the easiest way to triangulate what the smart money already believes about a company.

What I look for:

1. **Who led**. Was the lead a fund with a real track record in this sector and stage, or a generalist trying to learn the category? Sector specialists who lead are betting their reputation. Tourists are betting a small piece of fund.

2. **Who follow-on**. Existing investors not following their pro rata is the single loudest signal in venture. It is usually whispered between the lines of "we're conserving for our reserve pool" or "we're past our check-size window". Read those carefully.

3. **Angel signal**. Operator angels who have built companies in the same sector are extremely high signal. Tourist angels — actors, athletes, generic high-net-worth — are zero signal at best and negative signal at worst when they crowd out real participants.

4. **Strategic investors**. A strategic on the cap table is sometimes a green flag (channel access, design-partner customer) and sometimes a poison pill (the strategic will block an acquirer in a future round). Diligence the specific strategic; do not generalize.

5. **The partner, not the firm**. "Sequoia is on the cap table" is meaningless. "Sequoia's Pat Grady is on the cap table" is meaningful. Funds are bundles of partners. Some partners are 10x better than the fund average. Some are dragging it.

6. **What's missing**. Who is suspiciously *not* on the table? If every major SaaS investor passed on a seemingly hot SaaS deal, that is informative. Diligence the passes, not just the participants.

The cap table tells you what smart people who saw this deal first believed when they decided. Your job is to figure out whether their information set was complete, and whether anything has changed since they signed.
