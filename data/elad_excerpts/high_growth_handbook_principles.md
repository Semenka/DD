# High Growth Handbook — distilled principles

The job of the CEO scales with the company. Early on you are doing every job badly. By Series B you are managing managers. By Series C you are setting strategy and recruiting executives. Most founders fail because they keep doing the job that worked at the previous stage instead of the job the company now needs.

Hiring beats coaching. By the time you realize someone is a B-player on your executive team, the cost has already compounded across their org. The lag between "I am worried about X" and "I should fire X" is the most expensive lag in a startup.

The single highest-leverage activity for a founder is recruiting executives. Plan for it years in advance. Build relationships with potential VPs over coffee for 18 months before you need them. Do not run the search when the seat is already empty.

Pattern matching is a tool, not a religion. Most successful companies look weird to investors. The job is to look at the weird company and decide which weird parts are signals and which are noise.

When fundraising, the deck is the deal. Investors decide in the first five slides. Spend disproportionate time on the slides that establish (1) who you are, (2) what you sell, (3) why now.

Inflection over TAM. A 10 billion market that is shrinking 5% a year is a worse bet than a 200 million market growing 80%. The question is not "how big is this" but "what is changing right now that makes this category exist".

The board's job is not to make decisions. The board's job is to ratify the CEO's decisions, fire the CEO when they have lost the team, and to be useful between meetings. Founders who treat the board as a decision-making body lose the company.

Most VC value-add is signaling. The real value-add of a great VC is (1) pattern recognition on the next executive hire, (2) defending the CEO in tough boardroom conversations, (3) backchanneling on competitors. Anything else is gravy.
