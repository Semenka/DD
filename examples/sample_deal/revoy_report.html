Synthesis

Exec summary

Revoy is a bridge-electrification bet for U.S. long-haul trucking, not a broad Class 8 market bet. [12][ref: market_inflection.md]

The wedge is dense diesel lanes where a swappable hybrid dolly avoids new tractor and trailer capex. [9][12]

The market thesis is plausible because BEV replacement is slow, but the economics depend on lane utilization and swap infrastructure density. [9][12]

The deal is not underwritable today because ARR, growth, margin, retention, customer count, and founder data are missing. (DealContext)

The cap table gives YC signaling, but no disclosed domain lead, fleet investor, OEM, or strategic investor validates the next phase. [61][63][64]

Beliefs Required to Invest

  1. We must believe that 170-250 mile diesel lanes can support enough repeat volume for sub-5-minute swaps and high charger utilization. [9][12]
  2. We must believe that fleets delay full BEV tractor replacement long enough for Revoy to become the bridge standard. [10][12]
  3. We must believe that Revoy can finance hardware and infrastructure growth without utilization misses destroying unit economics. [9][12]
  4. We must believe that missing traction data will show Series A quality revenue, growth, margin, retention, and customer concentration. (DealContext)
  5. We must believe that the absence of a disclosed trucking, OEM, fleet, climate, or deep-tech lead is not a negative signal. [61][64][ref: coinvestor_lens.md]

Kill Shot

The cross-cutting kill shot is that this is a capital-intensive lane-density infrastructure bet being priced before the core proof is visible. Principle 1 applies. The market only works on dense lanes with high utilization and roughly 24 Revoys per commercial lane. [9] The traction file has no ARR, growth, gross margin, retention, customer count, or reverse DCF. (DealContext) The founders section has no founder data. (DealContext) The cap table shows YC and TRAC, but no disclosed domain lead or strategic customer-channel investor. [61][63][64] This can fail even if the product works.

1-line bet

Pass until lane utilization, revenue quality, founders, and domain-led capital prove this is not infrastructure risk.

Recommendation

Pass for now — revisit at signed commercial lane deployments with ARR, gross margin, utilization, retention, founder data, and strategic lead evidence.

Market

Inflection thesis

The inflection is U.S. long-haul trucking moving into a bridge-electrification phase before full BEV replacement works at fleet scale. EPA’s March 2024 heavy-duty rule expects 25% of long-haul truck sales in 2032 to be ZEVs, while charging gaps and battery-swapping interest are rising in the U.S. [12].

Principle 2 applies: the bet is not Class 8 TAM, but dense diesel lanes where a swappable hybrid dolly beats new-truck capex [ref: market_inflection.md][12].

Sizing

  • Today: The closest cited analyst market is global Class 8 trucks at $337.50B in 2025 and $353.80B in 2026. The U.S. Class 8 market is $51.17B in 2026 [10]. This is not Revoy SAM because it includes diesel, BEV, hybrid, hydrogen, tractor, rigid, dump, and vocational trucks [10].
  • Five-year: The same report gives $539.26B global Class 8 trucks by 2034 at 5.4% CAGR [10]. The 2031 implied market is $460B using the cited 2026 base and 5.4% CAGR. Growth drivers are freight demand, e-commerce, infrastructure, and electrification [10].
  • Wedge to serve: Revoy’s first wedge is repeatable U.S. long-haul diesel lanes of roughly 170-250 miles, where swaps take under 5 minutes and high charger utilization drives economics [9][12]. This is lane density, not all trucking.
